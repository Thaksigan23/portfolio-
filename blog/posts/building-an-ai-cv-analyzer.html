Building an AI-Style CV Analyzer

2026-06-25 · Engineering

The feature I'm most proud of in CareerGuide Hub is the AI CV Analyzer. A student uploads a résumé and instantly gets a skill-based analysis, a match score, and a ranked list of jobs that fit them. This post walks through how I approached it—and the trade-offs I made to keep it fast and reliable.

The goal

A CV is a static document. I wanted to turn it into something actionable: extract the skills, compare them to live job postings, and surface the best matches with a clear percentage—so a student knows exactly where they stand and what to improve.

How it works

  • Upload & parse: the CV is uploaded with Multer and the text is extracted on the server
  • Skill extraction: the text is normalized and matched against a curated skill vocabulary
  • Scoring: each job is scored by how many of its required skills the candidate has, weighted by relevance
  • Ranking: jobs are sorted by match percentage and returned with a "Top Match" badge for the best fit
  • History: every analysis is saved so students can track progress as they update their CV

Why a transparent score beats a black box

I deliberately kept the matching logic explainable. Instead of an opaque model, the score reflects concrete overlap between CV skills and job requirements. That means a student can see why a job is a 100% match versus a 40% one—and what skill would move the needle. For a career tool, trust and clarity matter more than sophistication for its own sake.

Challenges

Messy input was the hard part. Résumés come in wildly different formats, so the parser had to be forgiving—handling inconsistent spacing, synonyms (e.g. "JS" vs "JavaScript"), and noise. I solved this with a normalization step and a synonym map before scoring, which dramatically improved match accuracy.

What I learned

  • Designing an explainable scoring algorithm that users can actually trust
  • Handling file uploads and server-side text extraction safely
  • Turning a one-off result into a saved, trackable history with PostgreSQL
  • That a simple, transparent approach often delivers more value than a complex one
